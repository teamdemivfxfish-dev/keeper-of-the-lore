Chapter 1: The Constant and the Coin
efore there were years to number them by, before there was a
single thing set apart from any other thing, there was the
Constant. It was not a god, for a god must have something
outside itself to look upon, and the Constant had nothing; it was all
things at once, seamless and whole and entirely alone. It knew itself
the way a sleeper knows that he is sleeping and cannot wake, and for
an age past all measuring that was enough. And then, by the slow
degrees with which all great changes come, it was not enough.
Perfection has no future, because it has nowhere left to go; and so the
Constant came at last to a longing it had no name for and no means to
answer. It wished to move. It wished to be answered. It wished,
though the word for it would not exist until long after, to be alive.
B
There was only one thing so vast a being could do to make a
second thing where there had only ever been one. It tore itself apart.
Out of that first and greatest of all wounds came two. The one was
a woman woven of light and motion and the green rush of all
beginnings, and she named herself Luminara. The other was a man of
cool and quiet and the long grey patience of endings, and he named
himself Mortanis. They had been a single thing a moment before, and
the parting had not healed them; it had only made them two. So the
first act of each was to turn and look upon the other; and the second
was to reach across the new and terrible distance the tearing had
opened between them; and the third, for they had nothing else in all
of creation to do, was to begin to dance. For an age that was the whole
of existence: the two of them turning together in the dark, the light

3
and the dusk of the same lost coin, and neither one asked for anything
more, because there was nothing more in all the dark to ask for.
It was Luminara who first grew restless, as she would always be
the first to grow restless. She wished for company smaller than a god,
things that she might shape and watch and love, and she spoke of it to
Mortanis in the dark. He did not understand what she meant by it; he
had never wanted anything in his existence but her, and could not see
why she should want for anything else when she had him. But he had
learned already the one thing that would define him to the very end of
the world, which was that he could refuse her nothing. He agreed
because she wished it, and not because he understood it; and that
habit of his heart, that long and sweetly foolish blindness toward the
one he loved, was set hard in him before there was ever a world for it
to break upon. Remember it. Everything that follows turns upon it in
the end.
So Luminara made the first man. She shaped him whole and
flawless and bright, Primeus she named him, the Perfect One, and set
him down to be admired. And Mortanis, watching her, was moved to
make something of his own, and found that he could not. He reached
down into himself for the breath of life and there was none of it there;
all that came out of his hands was matter. He made the ground, and
the grey weight of the mountains, and the deep and silent reach of the
sea. He could make the shore. He could never once make the thing
that would walk upon it. So he did the only thing left to a maker who
has no life in his hands to give: he made the world beautiful, and he
made all of it in her image. He drew the long red seam of the horizon
because it was the color of her at evening. He hid bright ores in the
black of the deep earth because they had the gleam of her smile. He
set the sea forever turning against the shore in the rhythm of the
dance the two of them had danced when there had been nothing else
in all the dark to do. And Luminara walked out across the whole of it
and breathed, and his bare mountains filled with her birds, and his
empty sea with her fish, and his still grey grass with her cattle; and the
two of them stood together over a finished and a living world, his
bones and her breath, and there has never since been anything in all of
time as whole as the two of them were in that single hour.

4
Only Primeus did not move. The Perfect Man stood where she had
set him and looked upon nothing and reached for nothing, and the
longer the two gods watched him stand alone in the midst of their
paradise, the colder they grew; for they knew that stillness. They had
felt it themselves before the Constant broke, and it had been
unbearable even to a god. So they did to the first man the very thing
that had been done to them. They took Primeus and split him down
the center into two, into a man and a woman; and the two halves
woke and turned at once toward one another, and reached, and held
on, and did not let go. From that holding came every mortal thing that
has ever lived, and died, and feared the dark between.

Chapter 2: The Gift and the Grief
he first man and the first woman bore children, and their
children bore children, and in the turning of a few short ages a
world that had been made for two stood loud with thousands.
They raised walls and roads and small bright kingdoms and smaller
bitter quarrels. They were brief, and quick, and altogether unlike the
great slow gods who had made them, and Luminara loved them past
all reason, in the way that the very young love the first thing they
have ever shaped with their own two hands.
T
But the bodies she had given them carried a flaw she had never
thought to look for, because she had only ever learned how to begin a
thing and never once how to end one. They aged. Their straight backs
bent and their bright eyes clouded, and the quick glad lives she had
made for them turned slow and sour with pain. And the first man and
the first woman, who had lived the longest of all, came to her at the
last and begged her to let them stop. She could not. She stood over the
two she had shaped with her own hands and found that for all the
light in her she had no mercy to give them; she had only more life, the
very thing that had become their torment, and she wept, and could do
nothing at all.
Mortanis could. He came in the dark, where she would not have to
watch him do it, and knelt beside the old man whose every breath had
become a labor, and laid a cool hand upon a brow that had known ten
thousand mornings, and let the long day end. It was the first death in
the world, and it was the gentlest thing that had ever been done in it.
He had found at last the half of the making that she had always
lacked: she could open the door of a life, and he could close it again
softly behind, and between the two of them lay the whole of it. And
what he did in the dark that night did not stop with the one old man.
It became the deepest of all the laws, the one that lies beneath the
others, the one neither of them would ever afterward be able to
unmake however bitterly they came to wish it; that what begins must
end, and what ends must one day return. In a far later age, when there
were priests left to give such things their names, they would call that
law the Cycle, and they would build their whole understanding of life

6
and death upon it, never once guessing it had been born of a single act
of mercy in the dark.
Luminara did not call it mercy. She called it murder. She struck
him across his grey calm face, and she fled from him down into the
loud bright tangle of her children, and she did not come back. There
among the mortals she stayed, and they adored her, for she shone
among them like a morning come down to walk the earth; and in the
long years of her exile she took comfort, and bore children of her own
by mortal fathers, and those children carried the light of a goddess in
their blood. They were the first of the Elves.
Because a goddess had borne them, the Elves grew up certain that
they stood above every other living thing that breathed, and they
were not wrong by very much, and it was the ruin of them. Nor did
they long stay one people. Those whose blood ran closest to their
mother's, the proudest and the fewest of them, named themselves the
High Elves, and held all the rest beneath their notice. Others mingled
their blood freely with the short-lived race of men and grew the more
numerous for it; and these, in a later age, would learn to wed and to
bear children under the turning tides of the moon, by rites the High
Elves found shameful to speak of, and so would come to be called the
Moon Elves. Others still went down into the deep green forests and
gave themselves over to the wild until bark grew where their skin had
been and sap ran where their blood had run, and these were the Wood
Elves. The three kinds despised one another in the particular and
patient manner that only kin can ever truly manage, and Luminara,
watching her own children sort themselves so eagerly into a long
ladder of disdain, was charmed by the spectacle of it; and that small
charmed cruelty was the first thing she ever made that she could not
afterward take back.
Mortanis walked among the mortals too, in those years, searching
every road and shore for her. But where she came among them like a
morning, he carried the cold of the grave upon him and the smell of
all endings, and they feared him on sight and would not suffer him
near. Luminara, who could have told them the truth of him with a
single word, told them instead only the half of it that suited her grief.
She taught them that he was the thief who comes in the night, the

7
ender, the maker of the drowning sea and the killing mountains; and
she did not tell them that the sea and the mountains had been his
love-gifts to her, nor that on the worst of her nights she still walked
out alone past the last of the firelight and stood a long while in the
cold and the dark and said nothing, and came back with her eyes red,
and let no one ask her why. The mortals, having buried their own
fathers and needing a face to fix upon the dark, listened to her and
learned to hate him; and at the last they began to wonder, in the low
and careful voices that men keep for their very worst thoughts,
whether the world might not be a cleaner place with no god of death
left in it at all.

elves, who had inherited from their mother both her pride and her

A
The shaping of it fell to the elves, for the weaving of whole new
realms out of nothing was a gift their mother's blood had left in them.
They wove a place that was not a place at all; an emptiness with no
floor beneath it and no roof above it and no single point of light
within it, a pure and seamless and perfect nothing. And they did not
know, or did not let themselves care, that in doing so they had made
the one cage in all of creation that could truly hold him. For the
emptiness they wove was the very same emptiness that had bound
Luminara and Mortanis together before there was ever a world; it was
the old aloneness of the Constant before it broke; and there was
nothing in all his long grey existence that the god of death feared as he
feared being put back down into that.
The luring of him fell to the humans, and here they proved
cleverer than they had any right to be, for they had stumbled, without
ever understanding why, upon the single truth about the god of death
that could be made to undo him. He could not be taken by force, for
he was the end of all force that there was. He could not be taken by
any lie told about the world, for he saw the plain truth of all worldly
things the instant he looked upon them. There was only one subject in
the whole of existence upon which his clear cold sight had ever failed
him, one place where the wisest of all the deathless powers became as
blind and as witless as the greenest of mortal boys. It was her. And so
that, of course, was exactly where they struck.

9
They chose out a handful of bold men, and set among them a few
of the fairest of the elves, that the thing might carry the weight of his
own beloved's children in its faces; and they sent these to him as
messengers, and they told him the one story that he had wanted,
above all the stories in the world, to be true. They told him that
Luminara was weary. That she grieved, in secret, for the world that
feared and reviled the husband she loved. That she wished at last to
set down the long unhappy business of being worshipped, and to
leave the noise and the press of the mortals behind her, and to
withdraw with him to a quiet place apart, the two of them wholly
alone together as they had been in the dark before ever the world was
made. They told him she had bidden them go ahead of her and make
such a place ready, far from all of it; and that she would come to him
there, when it was finished, and that she had asked of him only this
one small thing, that he go before her and wait.
A colder god would have looked hard at the door before he set his
foot across it. A wiser one would have stopped to ask why his wife,
who could find him unerring in the dark of the deepest sea, should
have any need of borrowed mortal mouths to send for him at all.
Mortanis did neither of these things. He had spent a long and bitter
age being hated for the single kindness he had to give, and he wanted
nothing in all the wide turning world so much as to be alone with her
again; and so when the messengers brought him to the threshold of
that empty place, he did not study it, and he did not question it. He
stepped across it gladly, with something stirring in his grey old heart
that was very near to joy, to go and make ready a quiet room for a
wife who had never once sent for him.
The door shut behind him.
And in the silence that came after, Mortanis understood two things
in the very same instant, and both of them far too late. The first was
that the emptiness now closing in upon him from every side was the
old loneliness from before the world, the one thing he had feared
above every other thing there was. The second was that no one had
sent for him; that no one was coming; that he had walked down into
his own grave upon his own two feet, for love, like the youngest and
most foolish of all mortal men. The deceivers slipped quietly away

10
while he stood there in the dark, back to the bright safe world that had
set them to the task, and the world remembered them for it ever after
by a single cold word. It called them revenants. There were not many
of them, all told. One among them, ten thousand years and more
beyond that night, would come to matter more than all the rest of his
kind together, more by far than he himself could have dreamed as he
stood in the firelight telling a lie to a god; but that is very far ahead of
this telling, and there would be no living soul left by then who
remembered his face.
The grief in Mortanis turned to rage, because there was nothing
else in that place for it to turn to, and nowhere at all for it to go.
Molten earth began to weep from his body. Fire ran from his eyes and
would not be stopped. The empty prison filled by slow degrees with
the fury of a betrayed and a humiliated god, until the nothing itself
caught and burned, and the seamless dark became a vast and lightless
and screaming country of flame; and the elves who had woven it,
looking long afterward upon what their clever cage had made of itself,
gave it the name that it bears to this day. They called it the Nether.
Far above, in the bright untroubled world, Luminara felt him go
out of it the way a watcher feels a great fire snuffed in a single breath,
and she felt in the same moment the first cold crack open somewhere
deep in the center of herself. The seasons would no longer turn for
her. The fields would not die back, and so could not be born again.
The dying could not die, and lay in their beds in every land begging
for the rest that there was no longer anyone left in all the world to give
them. She did not understand any of it; she knew only that something
at her very core had broken and would not mend. And worst of all,
she did not know what had been done, nor in whose name it had been
done. No one ever told her that her beloved had walked smiling into
the dark in the belief that she herself had called him there. She would
not learn the truth of it for an age. And when at the last she did learn
it, it was no comfort to her at all.

Chapter 4: The Long Search and the Festering
Wound
he crack in her widened into a ruin. Luminara went down to
the very mountains that Mortanis had raised for her and beat
her divine head against them until the high peaks rang like
struck bells. She walked out into the cold grey sea he had shaped for
her and let it close over her, searching the black water for any last
trace of him, and came up with her hands empty, and screamed, and
went under once again. She dug into the earth he had made and
smeared herself with its dust and could not find him anywhere in it.
And from the fractures that open in a breaking mind, pieces of her
began to come loose and stand up, alive.
T
Two of those pieces would matter above all the rest. From her
knowing there came Xerion, cold and exact and bottomless, who
looked upon the wreck of his mother and saw at once a problem that
had a solution somewhere within it. And from her imagining there
came Forgrimm, vast and loud and warm as a forge in winter, who
looked upon that same wreck and wept openly, and then rolled up his
enormous sleeves and set to work. They were brothers, the two of
them, struck from the one same shattering, and they were not alike in
any single other thing; and between the two of them, the cold one and
the warm one, they would carry the unbearable grief of the gods for
an entire age. Mark them both well. They are not done with this tale,
nor it with them, by ten thousand years.
Xerion tended their mother with the patience of a scholar and the
steady hands of a surgeon. He drew up what faint lingering warmth
of Mortanis still clung to the soil and the sea and the deep ores, and
gave it back to her by slow careful measures; and in the doing of it he
learned the rites that held her splintering mind from coming wholly
and finally apart. But the warmth was running out, for its only source
lay buried in a burning prison, and Xerion's labor spent it faster than
the world could replace it. So Forgrimm did a thing that even his cold
brother named madness to his face. He took a blade to his own legs,
and out of the marrow of his shins and his thighs he carved small

12
stubborn creatures, shortening himself forever so that ever after he
would stand on truncated pillars where a god's long stride had been;
and into those small creatures he breathed the one purpose he had to
give them. They were to go down into the deep dark earth, and dig,
and dig, and dig, until they had found the lost god of death and
brought him home again to his wife. They were the Dwarves, the
carved children of the smith-god Forgrimm, and they took up their
picks and went down into the deep; and they were digging there still
when the bright world far above them had long since forgotten that
there had ever been any other way for a people to live.
What the Dwarves dug up, in the end, was not Mortanis himself
but the buried truth of him: that it was the humans and the elves who
had put him into the ground. They came up out of the deep and
demanded the road to the Nether and the magic that had built it. The
humans, frightened and long since sick with their own old shame,
gave up to them everything they knew. But the High Elves, far too
proud to be questioned by squat creatures made of marrow and dirt,
would not so much as speak with them, and turned them away from
their shining gates unanswered; and the hatred that was born
between dwarf and elf in that single hour has never once cooled in all
the long ages that have followed it, and it will come, before the end of
this telling, to matter more than either of those proud peoples could
ever have guessed.
From the magic that the humans had surrendered, the Dwarves
forged the first of the Netherportals, a single doorway cut into the side
of the burning country, and they passed through it. They had been
told to look for an empty dark. They found instead a hell; a vast and
lightless and screaming hell, the rage of a god worked into the very
shape of a landscape, and they did not turn back from it. They walked
that hell for a hundred years and more. They raised black fortresses in
the heart of the fire and buried their own dead in its ash, and they
searched every burning corner of a realm that did not wish to be
searched, until at the very last they found him, sitting alone and silent
in the molten heart of his own long fury.
They told him, in their plain and blunt and unlovely way, that his
wife was dying for the want of him, and that they had spent a

13
hundred years walking through hell only to come and say it to his
face. And the rage went out of the god of death all at once, the way a
great forge goes cold and dark in the moment the fire is finally let to
die; and where his burning heart had been there was nothing left but
the old grief, and a terrible new gratitude toward the small stubborn
folk who had come so far to find him. He rose. The ash froze hard
where his fiery tears had fallen. And he followed the Dwarves up out
of the dark and across the long cold neglected world, and when at last
he came to the place where Luminara sat broken and muttering to
herself in the dust, the god of death knelt down low before her, and
asked her to forgive him for having been fool enough to be deceived.
She did not understand him at first, for her mind by then was a
shattered and a scattered thing, and she had spent the whole of an age
believing only the one cruel simple story, that he had abandoned her.
But as she came slowly back to herself in the warmth of him, she
learned the whole of the truth at last; that he had gone down into the
dark of his own glad will, because mortal messengers wearing the
faces of her own children had told him that she had called him away
to a quiet place apart. And he learned, in that same hour, the matching
half that he had never known; that she had never once sent for him at
all, that the words had been put into her mouth by men who only
wanted him gone. The two of them wept then, the light and the dusk
together, for the same single lie, from the two far ends of an age. And
Mortanis took her ruined face between his two grey hands and told
her the only thing in all of it that had ever truly mattered, which was
that he had not, in the whole of his long imprisonment, been angry at
her, not for one instant. He had been angry at the dark, and at the
deceivers, and most of all at his own foolish loving heart; but never,
never, at her.
Luminara, for her own part, was not nearly so quick to forgive the
children who had done it. She made one last law before she let the
world fall altogether out of her thoughts. No mortal, she decreed,
would ever again come easily to the rest that her husband gives.
Every soul that died would first be made to pass through the Nether,
through the very burning country that her children had built to cage
their god, before it might be permitted to find its peace at last. And so

14
the prison that had been raised to keep him from her became instead
the long dark road that every mortal thing must walk to reach him;
and it is that road still, and will be until the Cycle itself is broken.
There was one thing more. It was the worst thing of all, though it
made no sound, and no one but Mortanis ever saw it happen. When
he held her ruined face between his hands and looked, in the way that
only a god may look, into the deep place behind her eyes, he saw that
not all of the breaking in her had healed. Down in the dark at the
bottom of her mind a small wrongness had opened itself while he was
gone, a thing with no floor to it and no bottom, and it was still, very
slowly and very patiently, spreading. He understood, looking at it,
exactly what it would one day grow to become. And he understood,
in the very same breath, precisely what he would have to do to close
it; how deep, and how cruelly, he would have to cut into the one thing
in all of creation that he could not bear to see hurt. His hand was
already moving to begin. He stopped it. He told himself that there
would be time enough for it later. And he gathered her in against him
instead, and held her, and said no word at all of the thing that he had
seen; because she was warm again, and quiet again, and his again,
and he had only just that hour got her back, and he was so very tired
of being alone. The wrongness behind her eyes went on widening in
the dark, patient and bottomless, and in a far age it would be given a
name, and a thousand horrors would come crawling out of it to
unmake the world. But all of that is yet to come. For now there was
only the two of them, broken, and together again; and the
long-neglected world turned on without them, into the age of mortal
men.

Chapter 1: The City
en thousand years and more passed over the world while the
two gods sat folded into one another in the dark and
remembered nothing of it. The seasons, left to themselves,
learned again to turn; the dying, after the long road through the
Nether, learned again to find their rest; and on the cooled and quiet
wreckage of a paradise that no one remembered, the race of men
came up out of the ground like grass after a long winter. They did not
know what they were standing on. They knew the hunger in the belly
and the itch in the hands, and that was enough to build a world upon.
T
They built one city before they thought to build any other, on a
wide plain of pale stone beneath an open sky, and they did not give it
a name, for there was as yet nothing to tell it apart from; it was simply
the City, the only one there was, and a man who said that he was
going to the City said it the way another might say he was going
home. It grew rich and loud and proud, a great circle of trade where
every road in the young world seemed at last to end. And out of its
restless crowds there rose five men in whom, though not one of them
knew it, the last thin embers of the old divinity still burned.
There was Fiesh, the eldest, who did not age and seldom slept, to
whom every man with a grievance came in the end, because he could
be trusted to find the fair line through any quarrel; and yet for all his
patience there was a heaviness behind his eyes, the look of a man
carrying a debt he cannot name and pays down a little with each

16
kindness he does. There was Perma, square and grey and certain, who
wrote the City's laws and laid its foundation-stones, and held to the
floor of his soul that a good rule honestly kept was the one thin wall
standing between men and ruin. And there were three younger and
hungrier than these. Ghust, who burned at everything and could not
abide to sit still. Bjorn, whose laughter was thunder and whose
temper was the lightning after it. And Tad-Wreath, slow and deep
and honest, who said little, and meant the whole of the little he said.
The world that the men of the City had been born into was a
thinning one, though they did not know the reason for it. The ores ran
out. The deep veins their grandfathers had worked gave up a little
less with every passing year, for the one who had long ago hidden
that wealth away in the dark, as a lover hides gifts, had been gone
from the world an age, and what he had buried was very nearly spent.
And the Five, who had built the whole of their greatness upon the
certainty that there was nothing in the world they could not have if
only they reached far enough for it, did not take kindly to an earth
that had at last begun to tell them no. So they reached down.
They sank shafts beneath the foundations of their own City, deeper
than any had ever dug before, chasing rumors of a great wealth that
lay still further down; and far below the last of the worked stone they
came upon a warmth that had no business living in the cold of the
deep, a slow red heat that seemed, almost, to breathe. They took it for
the glow of some vast unclaimed lode. It was not. It was the
long-dreaming edge of a wrongness that had been settling down into
the roots of the world for an age, seeping out of a wound that none of
them could have begun to imagine; and it had begun, down there in
the dark, to dream of waking.
They woke it. What came up through the shafts that they had cut
was not ore, and was not a beast, but a horror given scale and breath
and a hunger as old as grief itself; and the men who lived to speak of
it afterward gave it the only name that would fit, Ignis-Aeterna, the
fire that does not die. It rose, and the City came down around it. The
pale stone ran like the wax of a candle. The towers folded down into
the streets.

17
Perma, the man of stone, was standing upon a floor that had
ceased to be a floor when a seam of fire opened beneath his feet, and
he was gone down into it before he could so much as cry out, and the
others gave him up for dead. The three younger ones fled through the
burning streets; and one of them, even in his terror, even as he ran,
found the cold presence of mind to catch a measure of the creature's
black blood in a flask and carry it out with him, and that one small
theft would father monsters for an age to come. Only Fiesh did not
run. He stood in the falling soot until it had greyed his shoulders,
because in all his long life running had never once undone a thing that
was already done, and he had learned, long ago, to stay and look
upon his ruins.
When the ash had at last settled, the survivors crawled out of it
grey as the dead, and made one another a vow over the bones of
everything they had built: never again, they swore, would they fall to
dragon fire. And on the strength of that vow they raised a new city
out of the ruin of the old; and this one they named, for they had
learned the bitter use of names. They called it Ashfell, and meant the
word for hope. And only then, with a second city standing and a first
one dead behind them, did men begin to speak of the dead one as the
First City; for a thing is only ever the first of its kind once there is a
second to set against it, and grief, like glory, gives out its names in
hindsight.
